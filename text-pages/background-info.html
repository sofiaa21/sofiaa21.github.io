Context

Our scope

Much like the Great Depression of the 1930s or the Great Inflation of the 1970s, the 2008 financial crisis remains a critical case study for economists and policymakers. This assignment investigates the localized impact of this recession on San Francisco, analyzing whether economic hardship overlapped with shifts in specific crime types — such as property, financial, or violent crime — using SFPD data from 2007 to 2017.

We believe this decade-long window allows us to observe both the immediate shock of the crisis and the long-term trends of the subsequent recovery. Our hypothesis is supported by several key findings:

  • Systemic impact: According to the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime, economic crises are historically linked to higher crime rates as social safety nets are strained.
  • Economic impact: Research from the Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco found that ten years after the crisis, U.S. GDP remained approximately 7% lower than projected — roughly $70,000 in lost lifetime income per American. (Britannica).
  • Labor market collapse: Between 2007 and 2009, approximately 7.5 million jobs were lost, doubling the unemployment rate to nearly 10% by 2010 (Britannica).
