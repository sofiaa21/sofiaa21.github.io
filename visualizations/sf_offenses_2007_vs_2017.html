Focus Study: Sex Offenses

The Changing Geography of Sex‑Related Crimes

Between 2007 and 2017, San Francisco experienced notable shifts in sex offense patterns — both in reported volume and neighborhood concentration. We want to see if the increased rate of these crimes is reflected on the spatial distribution.The maps below compare the density estimates for pre‑recession baseline (2007) and a decade later (2017).

Subcategories include forcible sex offenses, non‑forcible acts and human trafficking . Each map visualizes 5,000 randomly sampled incidents (for computational parity), with warmer colors indicating higher incident density.

Interpreting the Shift

What do the maps tell us? The 2007 density surface shows concentrated hot spots in the Tenderloin, South of Market (SoMa), and the Mission District — areas historically associated with higher crime reporting and street activity. By 2017, new clusters appeared further south and west, particularly around Bayview-Hunters Point and Visitacion Valley.

While some of the original hot spots persisted, this comparison shows that crime is not static. Post-recession period brought economic pressures - financial, housing, and social - that have impacted human behaviour and crime patterns all over the city.

Keep in mind: Around 2010, the police started to focus more on human trafficking and shifted policies around prostitution (which was counted in Public Order violations). In addition, a rise of online sex work and changing enforcement priorities may have influenced the spatial distribution.
